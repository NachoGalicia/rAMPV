The human pharmacokinetic profile of cyanocobalamin following parenteral administration is characterized by rapid systemic entry, extensive protein binding, and a highly efficient but saturable renal elimination mechanism. Following intravenous (IV) administration, the vitamin achieves immediate and complete systemic bioavailability, resulting in an instantaneous peak plasma concentration ($C_{max}$). In contrast, intramuscular (IM) and subcutaneous (SC) administrations introduce an absorption phase from the injection site, though both routes still yield a bioavailability approaching 100%. Peak plasma concentrations following IM or SC injection are typically reached within 30 to 60 minutes. While IV administration provides the most rapid increase in circulating levels, IM and SC routes are often clinically preferred for treating deficiency states, as the slightly more gradual entry into the bloodstream allows for a more efficient interaction with endogenous transport proteins before the renal threshold for "unbound" vitamin is exceeded.

Once in the systemic circulation, the distribution of cyanocobalamin is governed by its affinity for specific transport proteins, primarily transcobalamin II (TCII) and transcobalamin I (TCI, also known as haptocorrin). TCII is responsible for the active transport and receptor-mediated endocytosis of the vitamin into target cells via the CD320 receptor, whereas TCI serves as the primary circulating storage reservoir. The vitamin is rapidly sequestered into highly perfused organs, with the liver acting as the principal storage depot, containing approximately 50% to 90% of the total body pool. Significant concentrations are also distributed to the kidneys, bone marrow, and endocrine glands. Cyanocobalamin readily crosses the placental barrier and is secreted into breast milk, ensuring the transfer of essential cobalamin to the fetus and neonate. The biological half-life of stored vitamin B₁₂ in the liver is exceptionally long, measured in years, which accounts for the significant delay in the clinical manifestation of deficiency even after absorption ceases.

The metabolism and elimination of cyanocobalamin are dictated by the saturation of its transport proteins and its intracellular conversion into active coenzymes. Within the mitochondria and cytosol of target cells, the cyano-group is removed, and the cobalt atom is reduced to allow for the synthesis of the two coenzymatically active forms: methylcobalamin and adenosylcobalamin. Elimination occurs almost exclusively through the kidneys via glomerular filtration. Under physiological conditions, a significant portion of filtered cobalamin is reabsorbed in the proximal tubules; however, this reabsorptive capacity is limited. Following parenteral doses exceeding 100 to 500 µg, the amount of cyanocobalamin in the plasma far exceeds the binding capacity of the transcobalamins. This "excess" or unbound fraction is not reabsorbed by the kidneys and is rapidly excreted in the urine, with up to 50% to 90% of a 1,000 µg dose being eliminated within the first 48 hours. While a small fraction of cobalamin is excreted into the bile and undergoes enterohepatic circulation, this pathway is primarily essential for the conservation of dietary B₁₂ rather than the clearance of high-dose parenteral supplements.
