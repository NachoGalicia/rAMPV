1. Absorption and plasma profile

After intramuscular (IM) and subcutaneous (SC) administration, cyanocobalamin is rapidly and almost completely absorbed from the injection site, bypassing gastrointestinal mechanisms. Peak plasma concentrations are typically reached within ~30 minutes to 2 hours, with manufacturer data indicating peak levels within ~1 hour after IM injection.

The systemic availability following IM/SC injection is high, and absorption is not subject to intrinsic factor–dependent limitations.

Following intravenous (IV) administration, absorption is by definition immediate, producing a rapid rise in plasma concentrations; however, this route is associated with rapid renal clearance and a large fraction of the administered dose appearing in urine within a short time frame (often within 24–48 hours).

The plasma concentration–time profile is characterized by:

A rapid distribution phase shortly after parenteral dosing
Followed by a slower decline reflecting tissue uptake and storage

Overall, IM and SC routes provide a more sustained plasma exposure compared with IV administration, which leads to higher early peaks but faster decline due to urinary loss.

2. Distribution

Once in the systemic circulation, cyanocobalamin is extensively bound to plasma transport proteins, primarily:

Transcobalamin II (TCII) – delivers vitamin B12 to tissues (biologically active fraction)
Transcobalamin I (haptocorrin) – serves mainly as a circulating storage form

Cellular uptake occurs via receptor-mediated endocytosis of the TCII–cobalamin complex.

The distribution is characterized by:

Extensive tissue uptake, especially in the liver, which stores approximately 50–90% of total body vitamin B12
Additional storage in bone marrow, kidneys, and other tissues

The overall volume of distribution reflects both plasma protein binding and significant intracellular sequestration, resulting in a large apparent distribution space and prolonged retention in the body.

Importantly, there is significant enterohepatic circulation, with biliary secretion followed by reabsorption, contributing to maintenance of body stores.

3. Metabolism and elimination

Cyanocobalamin itself is not extensively metabolized in the classical sense, but is converted intracellularly into active coenzyme forms:

Methylcobalamin
Adenosylcobalamin

These active forms participate in essential enzymatic reactions (e.g., methionine synthase and methylmalonyl-CoA mutase).

Elimination occurs primarily via the renal route, with key features:

Approximately 50–98% of a parenteral dose may be excreted in urine, particularly in the early phase after administration
A substantial proportion of the dose is eliminated within the first 8–48 hours, especially after IV administration
Renal excretion involves filtration of unbound drug, with some tubular reabsorption

In addition:

Biliary excretion occurs (a few micrograms per day), with extensive enterohepatic recycling

The elimination profile is biphasic:

An initial phase reflecting plasma clearance (days)
A very prolonged terminal phase reflecting slow release from hepatic stores (months to years), explaining the long biological persistence of vitamin B12
Overall interpretation

In humans, parenteral cyanocobalamin displays rapid systemic availability (IM/SC) or immediate availability (IV), extensive protein-bound distribution with large hepatic storage, and predominantly renal elimination with marked early urinary loss and prolonged terminal retention.
