# Pharmacokinetic Data on Cyanocobalamin After Intramuscular, Subcutaneous, and Intravenous Administration in Laboratory Animals

## 1. Introduction

Cyanocobalamin (vitamin B12) is widely used in both clinical and research settings, with its pharmacokinetics varying significantly depending on the route of administration—intramuscular (IM), subcutaneous (SC), or intravenous (IV). Animal studies provide critical insights into absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion of cyanocobalamin, informing dosing strategies and therapeutic efficacy. Key findings indicate that IV administration leads to rapid peak plasma concentrations and a two-compartment kinetic model with extensive extravascular distribution in rats  (Nava-Ocampo et al., 2005). SC administration in cattle results in lower plasma and hepatic retention compared to hydroxocobalamin, with plasma levels peaking at 8 hours but returning to baseline within 24 hours  (Gonzalez-Rivas et al., 2021). Novel delivery systems such as dissolving microneedles have demonstrated enhanced systemic availability and sustained release compared to intraperitoneal injection in rats  (Bhowmik et al., 2025). Comparative studies also highlight differences between cyanocobalamin and other cobalamin analogues regarding tissue retention and bioavailability across species  (Kornerup et al., 2016; Kornerup et al., 2017; Rosenthal, 1962). These findings are essential for optimizing vitamin B12 supplementation protocols in veterinary medicine and experimental research.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Pharmacokinetics After Intravenous Administration

IV administration of cyanocobalamin in rats produces rapid peak plasma concentrations within minutes. The drug follows a two-compartment kinetic model with half-lives ranging from approximately 20–50 minutes; clearance rates are between 4.5–9 mL/min and volume of distribution at steady state ranges from 140–470 mL  (Nava-Ocampo et al., 2005). At high doses or prolonged exposure (up to 100 mg/kg for 26 weeks), non-linear kinetics may occur due to possible saturation of renal tubular reabsorption  (Nava-Ocampo et al., 2005).

### 3.2 Pharmacokinetics After Subcutaneous Administration

In cattle, a single SC injection of cyanocobalamin results in plasma levels peaking at around 8 hours post-administration but returning close to baseline within 24 hours; hepatic retention is minimal compared to hydroxocobalamin  (Gonzalez-Rivas et al., 2021). Similar patterns are observed in other species: tissue accumulation is generally higher in kidneys than liver after IM or SC injection  (Rosenthal, 1962), while oral bioavailability remains low unless permeability enhancers are used  (Chiang et al., 2020; Chiang et al., 2022).

### 3.3 Pharmacokinetics After Intramuscular Administration

IM injection leads to rapid absorption with peak plasma concentrations typically achieved within an hour; however, tissue retention varies by species and age. In rabbits, kidney accumulation exceeds that of liver following IM injection  (Rosenthal, 1962). In rats, novel delivery systems such as dissolving microneedles can achieve comparable Cmax values but provide more sustained release than traditional injections  (Bhowmik et al., 2025).

### 3.4 Comparative Bioavailability & Tissue Distribution

Comparative studies show that hydroxocobalamin generally has superior tissue retention—especially hepatic—compared to cyanocobalamin after parenteral administration  (Gonzalez-Rivas et al., 2021; Kornerup et al., 2016; Kornerup et al., 2017). In rats given labeled forms orally or parenterally, both forms are absorbed efficiently but distribute differently: more hydroxocobalamin accumulates in liver while more cyanocobalamin is found in brain and muscle  (Kornerup et al., 2017). Kidney uptake is consistently high across analogues but increases further under starvation conditions  (Rosenthal, 1962).

## 4. Discussion

The reviewed literature demonstrates that the pharmacokinetic profile of cyanocobalamin is strongly influenced by the route of administration across laboratory animal models  (Nava-Ocampo et al., 2005; Gonzalez-Rivas et al., 2021; Kornerup et al., 2016; Kornerup et al., 2017; Rosenthal, 1962). IV administration ensures immediate systemic availability but also rapid clearance; IM and SC routes provide slower absorption with variable tissue retention depending on species-specific factors such as age or nutritional status  (Nava-Ocampo et al., 2005; Gonzalez-Rivas et al., 2021; Rosenthal, 1962). Notably, kidney accumulation is prominent regardless of route but can be modulated by physiological states like starvation  (Rosenthal, 1962).

Comparisons between cobalamin analogues reveal that hydroxocobalamin offers greater hepatic storage than cyanocobalamin—a finding consistent across ruminants and rodents—which may have implications for long-term supplementation strategies  (Gonzalez-Rivas et al., 2021; Kornerup et al., 2016; Kornerup et al., 2017). Advances such as microneedle arrays or permeability enhancers show promise for improving systemic availability via non-traditional routes but require further validation before widespread adoption  (Bhowmik et al., 2025; Chiang et al., 2020; Chiang et al., 2022).

Methodological limitations include small sample sizes (especially for large animal models), lack of direct head-to-head comparisons between all three routes within single studies, and potential confounding from co-administered substances (e.g., trace minerals) or disease states.

## 5. Conclusion

The pharmacokinetics of cyanocobalamin are highly dependent on the route of administration in laboratory animals: IV provides immediate but short-lived systemic exposure; IM/SC offer slower absorption with variable organ distribution; novel delivery systems may improve bioavailability but need further study.

### Research Gaps

Despite substantial progress, gaps remain regarding direct comparative PK data for all three routes within single animal models—especially for non-rodent species—and long-term outcomes related to repeated dosing or disease-modified states.
