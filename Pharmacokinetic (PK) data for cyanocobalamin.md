Pharmacokinetic (PK) data for cyanocobalamin (Vitamin B12) in laboratory animals vary significantly depending on the species, the dosage, and the specific binding proteins (transcobalamins) available. Because cyanocobalamin is a water-soluble vitamin with high affinity for transport proteins, its parenteral PK is characterized by rapid initial distribution followed by either storage in the liver or rapid renal excretion.

### **1. Rats (Sprague-Dawley, Wistar)**
Rats are the most common model for high-dose cyanocobalamin PK studies.

*   **Intravenous (IV) Administration:**
    *   **Model:** Follows a **two-compartment kinetic model** with extensive extravascular distribution.
    *   **Half-life ($t_{1/2}$):** Approximately **20–50 minutes** for the terminal phase at high doses (1–100 mg/kg).
    *   **Volume of Distribution ($V_{ss}$):** Ranges from **140 to 470 mL** (in a standard rat model).
    *   **Clearance ($CL$):** Ranges from **4.5 to 9 mL/min**. 
    *   **Non-linearity:** At very high doses, a negative relationship between dose and normalized AUC is observed, likely due to the **saturation of renal tubular reabsorption**, which increases renal clearance.
*   **Intramuscular (IM) and Subcutaneous (SC):**
    *   **Absorption:** Rapidly absorbed from injection sites. 
    *   **$T_{max}$:** Peak plasma concentrations are typically reached within **30–60 minutes**.
    *   **Elimination:** Highly dependent on the saturation of binding proteins (Transcobalamin I and II). Doses exceeding the binding capacity are rapidly excreted in the urine (up to 90% within 48 hours).

### **2. Rabbits (New Zealand White)**
Rabbits are often used to study alternative delivery systems (e.g., buccal vs. IM).

*   **Intramuscular (IM) Administration:**
    *   **$T_{max}$:** Extremely rapid, reaching peak concentrations at approximately **15 minutes**.
    *   **$C_{max}$:** For a standard therapeutic dose (e.g., Neuroton formulation), $C_{max}$ values of approximately **109 ± 9 pg/mL** have been reported.
    *   **Distribution:** Concentration drops quickly after the peak (e.g., to ~43 pg/mL by 30 minutes), reflecting rapid tissue distribution and renal filtration.

### **3. Dogs**
*   **Intravenous (IV) Administration:**
    *   **$C_{max}$ and AUC:** Evolve proportionally to the dose for total cobalamins.
    *   **Excretion:** IV administration results in the most rapid renal excretion compared to other routes, with minimal opportunity for liver storage if the dose is high.
*   **Intramuscular/Subcutaneous (IM/SC):**
    *   **Bioavailability:** High ($F \approx 100\%$).
    *   **Retention:** Often compared to hydroxocobalamin; cyanocobalamin is generally **retained less effectively** in the body and liver than hydroxocobalamin because it has a lower affinity for plasma binding proteins.

### **Important Considerations for Lab Animals**
1.  **Protein Binding:** B12 pharmacokinetics are unique because the vitamin must bind to **Transcobalamin II (TCII)** to enter cells. In laboratory studies using high doses (mg/kg levels), the amount of cyanocobalamin far exceeds the available TCII binding sites, leading to "unbound" B12 that is cleared almost immediately by the kidneys.
2.  **Liver Storage:** The liver is the primary storage site (holding 50–90% of the body's B12). In animal models, parenteral administration (IM/SC) is more effective at replenishing these stores than IV, as IV administration often "spills" over the renal threshold before the liver can sequester the vitamin.
3.  **Hydroxocobalamin Comparison:** In almost all lab species (dogs, sheep, rats), **hydroxocobalamin** shows better tissue retention and a longer half-life than cyanocobalamin because it binds more strongly to plasma proteins.
